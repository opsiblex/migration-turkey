Turkey as a Migration Hub

This project explores Turkey’s dual role in migration dynamics: as a destination country for incoming migration and as a country of onward movement toward Europe.

The visualizations below show how migration flows change over time and how different countries become more or less important in specific years. The aim is to make these patterns transparent, comparable, and easy to explore through interaction.

This site presents selected data in a structured and accessible way so that users can examine developments independently.

Key observations: Inflows to Turkey

Regional concentration

Inflows to Turkey are not evenly distributed across the world. The strongest flows come mainly from geographically closer countries and from regions affected by instability and forced displacement.

Dominant role of Syria

Syria clearly dominates the inflow pattern over much of the observed period. This highlights how strongly conflict-driven migration shapes Turkey’s position as a receiving country.

New visibility after 2022

From 2022 onward, Ukraine becomes more visible in the data. This reflects how new geopolitical crises can quickly reshape migration patterns and add new source countries to an already complex regional picture.

Turkey’s role

The map shows that Turkey is not only a transit space between regions, but also an important destination country in its own right. Migration flows toward Turkey are shaped by war, proximity, and regional dynamics.

Transit · The Crossroads Effect

Turkey at the Crossroads

Turkey sits between two migration dynamics: it hosts millions of refugees fleeing conflict, while simultaneously serving as the main transit corridor for irregular crossings into the EU via the Eastern Mediterranean route. These two forces are deeply connected — as instability rises, both inflow and transit pressure increase together.

885K
Peak transit crossings
In 2015, 885,386 people crossed irregularly from Turkey into the EU via the Eastern Mediterranean route — the highest ever recorded. This coincided with Turkey hosting 2.5 million refugees, mostly Syrians fleeing the civil war.
98%
Drop after EU–Turkey deal
The EU–Turkey Statement of March 2016 dramatically reduced irregular crossings — from 885K in 2015 to just 42K in 2017, a drop of over 95%. Yet the number of refugees hosted in Turkey kept rising, showing that the deal stopped transit but not inflow.
2022
The economic crisis effect
As Turkey's economic crisis deepened in 2021–2022, both trends accelerated simultaneously: irregular crossings rebounded while Turkish citizens began leaving for Europe in record numbers — reflecting how domestic instability affects all migration flows.
3.7M
Refugees vs. transit — inverse link
Even as Turkey hosted a record 3.7 million refugees in 2021, transit crossings remained low due to the EU deal. This shows Turkey's unique role: absorbing enormous humanitarian pressure while acting as a buffer for the EU's external borders.

Outflow · Turkish Citizens in Europe

Where Do Turkish Citizens Go?

First residence permits issued to Turkish citizens by EU/EEA countries, broken down by reason. The data reveals a significant rise in outward migration — particularly for employment and family reasons — with Germany remaining the dominant destination throughout the observed period.

+117%
Rise in total permits
From 60,000 permits in 2015 to 130,000 in 2023 — a more than doubling of Turkish citizens receiving first residence permits in the EU, driven by economic instability and political uncertainty in Turkey.
+685%
Employment permits surge
Employment-based permits grew from 5,100 in 2015 to 40,100 in 2023 — a nearly sevenfold increase. The sharpest rise occurred between 2021 and 2022, coinciding with Turkey's currency crisis and high inflation.
#1
Family — largest single category
Family reunification consistently accounts for the largest share of permits — around 35% across all years. Germany alone issued 18,770 family permits to Turkish citizens in 2024, reflecting decades of established diaspora.
🇩🇪 34%
Germany dominates
Germany receives approximately one third of all Turkish outflow permits every year. Poland has emerged as the second largest destination since 2021, particularly for employment, reflecting new migration corridors in Eastern Europe.

Conclusion · A Call for Shared Responsibility

The Burden
is Real.
So Must
Be the Response.

Behind every data point in this analysis is a human being who was forced to leave their home — not by choice, but by war, persecution, or desperation. Turkey has absorbed nearly 3 million of them. That is not a statistic. It is the equivalent of an entire major city made up entirely of people who have lost everything.

885K

130K

The data tells a story that goes far beyond borders and numbers. Turkey is not simply a transit country. It is a nation that has taken on a humanitarian responsibility of historic proportions — hosting more refugees than any other country in the world — while simultaneously managing the political, economic, and social pressures that come with it.

"No people leave their homes unless home is the mouth of a shark."

— Warsan Shire, poet and refugee advocate

The 2015 crisis showed how quickly migration flows can reach breaking point when the international community fails to act early. The EU–Turkey deal of 2016 reduced irregular crossings dramatically — but it did not address the root causes of displacement. Turkey's refugee population kept growing even as transit numbers fell. The pressure did not disappear. It was simply absorbed — by Turkey alone.

At the same time, Turkey's own citizens are leaving. Over 130,000 first residence permits were issued to Turkish nationals by EU countries in 2023 — a 117% increase since 2015. This is not coincidence. Economic instability, currency collapse, and political uncertainty are pushing educated, skilled Turks westward. A country cannot sustain the world's largest refugee population while also experiencing its own significant brain drain without structural, sustained international support.

Three things the data demands we acknowledge
🌍
01 · Responsibility
Shared Burden,
Shared Future
Turkey absorbs a disproportionate share of global displacement. Sustained financial, diplomatic, and logistical support from the international community is not optional generosity — it is a structural necessity for regional and European stability. When a transit country is destabilised by the weight it carries, the consequences reach far beyond its borders.
🤝
02 · Integration
Integration is
an Investment
Research consistently shows that well-integrated migrants contribute positively to host economies — filling labour gaps, paying taxes, building communities. Failing to invest in language, employment access, and social inclusion does not save money. It creates long-term costs: for refugees, for transit countries, and for destination societies across Europe.
📊
03 · Transparency
Data is the
Antidote to Fear
Migration becomes a political weapon when facts are absent. Transparent, factual, accessible analysis is the first line of defence against fear-driven narratives that harm both migrants and host societies. Understanding the real numbers — who moves, why, and where — is not a political act. It is a democratic one.
"Turkey's burden is Europe's responsibility.
Migration's challenge is humanity's opportunity."

The flows will not stop. Conflict, climate, and inequality will continue to drive people across borders. The question is not whether migration happens — it is whether we respond to it with evidence, empathy, and shared commitment.
