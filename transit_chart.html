Transit · The Crossroads Effect

Turkey at the Crossroads

Turkey sits between two migration dynamics: it hosts millions of refugees fleeing conflict, while simultaneously serving as the main transit corridor for irregular crossings into the EU via the Eastern Mediterranean route. These two forces are deeply connected — as instability rises, both inflow and transit pressure increase together.

885K
Peak transit crossings
In 2015, 885,386 people crossed irregularly from Turkey into the EU via the Eastern Mediterranean route — the highest ever recorded. This coincided with Turkey hosting 2.5 million refugees, mostly Syrians fleeing the civil war.
98%
Drop after EU–Turkey deal
The EU–Turkey Statement of March 2016 dramatically reduced irregular crossings — from 885K in 2015 to just 42K in 2017, a drop of over 95%. Yet the number of refugees hosted in Turkey kept rising, showing that the deal stopped transit but not inflow.
2022
The economic crisis effect
As Turkey's economic crisis deepened in 2021–2022, both trends accelerated simultaneously: irregular crossings rebounded while Turkish citizens began leaving for Europe in record numbers — reflecting how domestic instability affects all migration flows.
3.7M
Refugees vs. transit — inverse link
Even as Turkey hosted a record 3.7 million refugees in 2021, transit crossings remained low due to the EU deal. This shows Turkey's unique role: absorbing enormous humanitarian pressure while acting as a buffer for the EU's external borders.
